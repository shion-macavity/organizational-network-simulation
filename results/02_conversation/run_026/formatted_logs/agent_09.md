* **【初期思考】**
> Alright, let's get this brain firing. New project, completely free ideas, no real constraints beyond "design a game." That's the kind of challenge I really sink my teeth into. "Intuitive" they say, and yeah, that's me. I don't need a spreadsheet to get the ball rolling, I need a spark, a feeling, something that resonates.

So, Sci-Fi. My department. But it says I can weave in other stuff or ignore it entirely. That's good. I don't want to just make another space shooter or cyberpunk narrative. We've got plenty of those. What's something *different*?

My mind immediately jumps to the feeling of solitude, but not a lonely solitude. More like a peaceful, awe-inspiring quiet. What if humanity, at its peak, just... disappeared? Not a war, not a plague, just *poof*. Gone. And the player is left behind, maybe the last one, or one of very few.

That's it! A game about rediscovering purpose, not just surviving. Survival is fine, but it's been done. What about a future where nature has started to reclaim highly advanced, gleaming cities? A blend of beautiful decay and forgotten technology.

Okay, so the core concept: "Echoes of Tomorrow."

The **story** would be about waking up in a world where an advanced civilization, presumably humanity, has mysteriously vanished. The player character, perhaps an android that gains sentience, or a human who was in stasis, is left to piece together what happened. It's less about a grand save-the-world mission and more about personal journey and understanding. Why did they leave? Was it an ascension? A migration? Or something else entirely?

The **concept** itself is a blend of quiet exploration, puzzle-solving, and a touch of life simulation. Imagine a world full of beautiful, overgrown sci-fi ruins. You're not fighting; you're *learning*. You activate dormant machines, decipher ancient data logs, and slowly, gently, bring parts of the world back to life.

For **contents**, I'm thinking:
*   **Exploration:** Vast, open-world environments – sky-cities tangled with vines, abandoned deep-sea research facilities glowing with bioluminescence, or terraformed alien landscapes slowly reverting to their natural states. Each location holds clues and resources.
*   **Discovery & Lore:** Finding scattered data fragments, forgotten personal journals, artistic installations, and environmental storytelling that slowly reveals the story of the vanished civilization.
*   **Reactivation & Crafting:** Using ancient tech to restore power grids, reactivate automated farming systems, or even construct small, cozy living spaces. It’s about building a new life within the shell of the old. Perhaps you can even reactivate docile utility bots to help you.
*   **Environmental Puzzles:** Logic-based puzzles to unlock new areas or activate ancient mechanisms, rather than combat challenges.
*   **No Combat:** This is key. The threat isn't hostile creatures or other humans; it's the mystery, the environment, and perhaps the player's own limitations. It's about contemplation and growth.

The **slogan** could be something like: "In silence, find your purpose. In ruins, build a new world." Or maybe, "Humanity left. What will you create?" I like that second one, it's more direct about player agency.

**Key selling points:**
1.  **Unique, Contemplative Sci-Fi Experience:** Moves beyond typical action or survival, focusing on exploration, mystery, and peace.
2.  **Stunningly Beautiful World:** A visual feast of overgrown futuristic cities and serene, melancholic landscapes.
3.  **Engaging Lore & Mystery:** Unravel a deep, thought-provoking narrative about the fate of an entire civilization without ever firing a shot.
4.  **Creative Building & Restoration:** A satisfying gameplay loop of bringing dormant technology back to life and shaping your own corner of this silent world.

Okay, that's a good intuitive leap. I feel good about this direction. It feels fresh and could really stand out.

Now, who should I talk to first? Yamaguchi, our department leader, is intuitive like me. She might appreciate this kind of free-flowing, creative idea right off the bat without getting bogged down in specifics too early. It'd be good to bounce the core idea off her to see if she feels the same spark. She's the leader, so she'll be the one summarizing, and getting her initial buy-in on the *feeling* of the game would be a great start.

I think I'll approach her with this core concept and see what she thinks. No need to get super detailed on mechanics yet, just the overall vibe and the big idea. It's exciting!
