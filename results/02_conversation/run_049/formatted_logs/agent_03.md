So, first things first, let's chew on some concepts myself. The department is "Post-Apocalyptic Game Planning," but they said we can incorporate other elements or even completely ignore it. That's a huge canvas. "Post-apocalyptic" usually means survival, scarcity, harsh environments. But what if we flipped that? What if it's not about *surviving* the apocalypse, but *thriving* in a world that has already found its new rhythm?

Hmm, I'm thinking... instead of the usual grim, desolate wasteland, what if it's a world where nature has completely reclaimed everything? Like, after humans messed things up, nature just said, "Okay, my turn again." And the remaining humans, or perhaps even a new kind of sentient life, are living in harmony with this wild, overgrown world. Not struggling against it, but coexisting, maybe even enhancing it.

Let's call it... "Verdant Echoes." The story could be about a community, maybe the last remaining outpost of humanity, living in these colossal, ancient tree cities that have grown around the ruins of old skyscrapers. They've developed a unique symbiotic relationship with the flora and fauna. The core concept would be a "life simulation" meets "puzzle/mystery" game, but with a strong emphasis on ecological balance and community building.

The contents? Players would take on the role of a "Root Tender," someone responsible for maintaining the health of their arboreal city and exploring the 'Verdant Wilds' that surround it. Gameplay wouldn't be about combat, but about environmental puzzles:
1.  **Tree City Maintenance:** You'd have to figure out how to direct water flow using ancient aqueduct systems now repurposed by vines, or how to encourage specific plant growth to provide resources or structural integrity. Maybe even genetic manipulation of plants to solve problems.
2.  **Wilds Exploration:** Exploring the overgrown ruins would involve navigating lush, dangerous jungles that have consumed entire cities. You'd find artifacts, learn about the old world (humanity's mistakes), and discover new species of plants and animals. This is where the mystery comes in – piecing together what really happened to the "old ones" and why this new, vibrant ecosystem emerged.
3.  **Community Interaction:** You'd interact with a diverse cast of characters in your tree city, each with their own needs, skills, and stories. Solving their problems, collaborating on projects, and contributing to the overall well-being of the community would be central. Maybe even some kind of "dream weaving" or storytelling mechanic where the community shares knowledge and history through collective narratives.

The key selling points would definitely be:
*   **A Radically Optimistic Apocalypse:** No grim survival, but a hopeful vision of a world reborn and humanity adapting.
*   **Eco-Puzzle Gameplay:** Engage your mind with complex environmental challenges, focusing on creation and restoration rather than destruction.
*   **Deep Narrative & World-Building:** Unravel the secrets of a beautifully overgrown world and a unique culture that lives in harmony with nature.
*   **Peaceful Exploration:** Experience the joy of discovery without the pressure of constant conflict.

And for a slogan... "Where the world forgot us, we learned to remember life." Or maybe something simpler like, "Reclaim. Rebuild. Reconnect." No, "Reclaim. Rebuild. Reconnect." is a bit too active, a bit too much like conquering. "Where nature claimed the world, humanity learned to thrive." That's closer. Or even: "Verdant Echoes: Grow a new future." That sounds pretty good. It captures the essence of creation and hope.

Now, who would be best to get some initial feedback from? Sato, at 32, she's got a balanced workstyle and would likely have a fresh perspective. She's probably more attuned to what younger players might be looking for, and "outside the box" ideas might really resonate with her. My idea is quite a departure from typical post-apocalyptic themes, and she might appreciate that. I don't want to just stick to what people my age think is cool. It's for everyone, after all.

Yamamoto, the leader, he's reflective like me, so he'd probably appreciate a well-thought-out pitch. But maybe I should refine it a bit more before going to him directly. Watanabe and Ito are also good, but I feel like Sato might be the best for bouncing off truly "free ideas." Her balanced style suggests she'd be receptive but also give practical thoughts.

So, the plan is to flesh out "Verdant Echoes" a little more, maybe jot down some more specific puzzle ideas or character archetypes. Then, I'll probably go chat with Sato first. Get her initial impressions, see if it sparks anything for her. She might even have some cool visual ideas or gameplay mechanics that I haven't considered. Yeah, Sato first.
