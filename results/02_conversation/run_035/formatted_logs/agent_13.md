* **【初期思考】**
> Alright, so here we are, another new project brief. "Design the story, concept, contents, slogan, and key selling points for a new product based on free ideas." And the genre assigned to our department, Crime & Investigation, is just a broad strategic direction. Wide open gameplay styles too. "Think outside the box," they said. Well, that's certainly a directive that gives us a lot of breathing room, which is good.

As the department leader, and a reflective one at that, my first instinct isn't to just jump into a big brainstorming session. I need to properly digest this brief myself first, really let it sink in. "Free ideas" can be a double-edged sword; it’s great for creativity, but without some initial structure or understanding of the *intent* behind that freedom, we could just end up with a chaotic mess.

So, 'who to consult first'? And 'how to approach this task'?

My internal process for something like this usually involves a bit of quiet contemplation before bringing the team in. I'm going to start by just *thinking*. What does "Crime & Investigation" mean to me in a really broad sense? It's not just about detectives and murders anymore, is it? It could be about uncovering any kind of truth, solving a mystery, understanding human behavior, perhaps even building a criminal enterprise or preventing one. The 'investigation' part could be scientific, historical, even interpersonal. And with "think outside the box," maybe we shouldn't even limit ourselves to traditional crime.

After I've had some solo time to mull over the *spirit* of the brief, I think the next logical step would be to involve the team, but not necessarily all at once in a formal meeting. Given the diverse workstyles and experiences we have, I want to tap into all of them effectively.

I'd probably start by sending out a brief internal message to the whole team – Nakamura, Kobayashi, Kato, Yoshida, Yamada, and Sasaki. I’d outline the project brief, emphasizing the "free ideas," the flexibility with genre and gameplay, and the encouragement to "think outside the box." I wouldn't ask for fully formed concepts immediately. Instead, I'd ask each of them to spend a bit of time, maybe a day or two, just jotting down any initial thoughts or keywords that come to mind. No pressure, no bad ideas, just raw input. What does "Crime & Investigation" *feel* like to them when they're told to ignore the usual constraints? What kinds of stories, characters, or mechanics spark their interest given this extreme freedom?

This way, everyone, regardless of their workstyle, has a chance to formulate their initial thoughts without being swayed by others or feeling the pressure of an immediate group discussion. Nakamura, being reflective like me, would appreciate the time. Kato and Sasaki, with their intuitive styles, might come back with some truly wild and wonderful initial sparks. Kobayashi and Yoshida, balanced as they are, might offer some foundational ideas or practical angles to consider even at this early stage. And Yamada, our youngest, could bring a fresh perspective on what's trending or innovative.

After collecting those initial individual thoughts, that's when I'd start thinking about who to have a more focused conversation with first. I'd review the raw ideas and see if any patterns emerge, or if there are any truly unique sparks. Based on that, I might pull in a smaller group – perhaps someone intuitive like Kato or Sasaki to help expand on a really abstract idea, or someone balanced like Kobayashi or Yoshida to help ground a wilder concept, or even Nakamura for a more in-depth, structured discussion.

I wouldn't directly consult the other department right off the bat. While the brief mentions it's an option, I feel it's crucial for *our* department to first establish our own initial direction and distinct ideas. We need to define what *our* unique take on "free ideas" means for Crime & Investigation. Once we have a more solidified internal concept, then perhaps an interaction with the other department could be valuable, either to provide a fresh perspective or to bounce a somewhat developed idea off them. But for now, it's about getting our internal gears turning and generating a wide breadth of options within our own team.

So, in short:
1.  My own reflection on the brief.
2.  Send out a general request to the team for initial, raw, unpressured thoughts and keywords.
3.  Review the team's initial inputs.
4.  Then, decide on smaller, focused discussions with specific team members based on their initial contributions to flesh out promising ideas.
