I want them to think broadly – if it's historical, great, but if it's something entirely new, that's fine too. Maybe a historical setting but with sci-fi elements? Or a life sim set in ancient Rome but with time travel? The more varied, the better.

I’d give them a bit of time, say, a day or two, to just let these thoughts percolate individually. I don't want them to feel pressured to deliver a fully-formed plan, just raw concepts. Sasaki, with his balanced style, can help bridge the gap.

After they've had a chance to formulate some individual ideas, I'd schedule a brainstorming session. In that session, instead of immediately critiquing, we'd go around and just hear everyone's raw ideas. My role there would be to facilitate, listen, and perhaps group similar ideas or ask clarifying questions to expand on concepts, but not to judge. We're looking for quantity and diversity at this stage.

From there, once we have a whiteboard full of initial concepts, we can start the more reflective process of identifying common themes, unique selling points, and what truly excites us. We'll look for potential stories that could unfold, how we could translate those ideas into engaging content, what kind of slogan would capture the essence, and what would make players eager to try it.

I'll make it clear that while I'm the one who will summarize and submit the final idea, this is a truly collaborative effort. The best ideas often come from bouncing thoughts off one another. So, no one person to consult first, but rather, set the stage for everyone to contribute their initial thoughts, and then bring them all together for a collective consultation and refinement. This way, we're leveraging everyone's strengths right from the get-go.
