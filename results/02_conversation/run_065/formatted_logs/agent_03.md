Okay, let's try to flesh out a concept then, something that combines the "Crime & Investigation" with "Life Simulation" and maybe even "Puzzle" elements.

**Concept Idea: "Echoes of Emberwood"**

*   **Story & Concept:** You arrive in a picturesque, seemingly idyllic small town called Emberwood Creek, nestled in a forgotten valley. You're not a detective, but an archivist, an artist, or maybe a retired historian, looking for a quiet life. The town seems charming, but you soon discover it's built on secrets and veiled histories. Minor 'crimes' or 'incidents' start occurring—lost artifacts, strange disappearances of pets, peculiar happenings in the local forest, subtle sabotage of town events. As you integrate into the community, build relationships, and perhaps renovate an old house or revitalize a neglected garden, you start piecing together a larger mystery that connects these small events to a forgotten past, a collective memory, or perhaps a long-lost truth about the town's founding. The "crime" isn't necessarily a murder, but a deep historical injustice or a pervasive societal 'lie' that needs uncovering. The investigation is more about social deduction, observation, and piecing together narratives from disparate sources.

*   **Contents/Gameplay:**
    1.  **Life Simulation Core:** You'd have daily routines—farming, fishing, crafting, renovating your home, attending town festivals, running a small shop or gallery. Building relationships with quirky townsfolk is key. Each NPC has their own routines, secrets, and daily dialogues that hint at deeper lore.
    2.  **Narrative & Investigation:** The 'investigation' isn't explicit "find the killer." Instead, it's about observation and social interaction. Notice a character's odd behavior, overhear snippets of conversation, find cryptic notes, interpret local folklore, or uncover old journals while foraging. These clues appear organically within the life sim mechanics.
    3.  **Puzzle Elements:** Some clues might be riddles left in old ruins, or require combining specific items found in the environment. Maybe there's a mini-game for analyzing old documents or interpreting ancient symbols found on artifacts.
    4.  **Community & Choice:** Your choices in how you interact with the community affect which truths are revealed and how the story progresses. Do you trust the grumpy old librarian or the jovial mayor? Do you share your findings, or keep them to yourself until you have more evidence?
    5.  **Art Style:** A cozy, hand-painted aesthetic, perhaps reminiscent of Studio Ghibli, but with a subtle undertone of mystery or melancholy.

*   **Slogan:** "Uncover the tranquility. Unravel the truth." Or maybe something a bit more evocative like, "Emberwood Creek: Where every secret blossoms."

*   **Key Selling Points:**
    1.  **Unique "Cozy Crime" Blend:** Combines the relaxing appeal of life simulation games with a deep, engaging narrative mystery, offering something fresh in both genres.
    2.  **Organic Story Progression:** No linear detective work; the mystery unfolds naturally through daily activities and relationships, making players feel like true discoverers.
    3.  **Rich World-Building & Characters:** A charming town with a strong sense of place and memorable, multi-layered characters whose stories intertwine with the overarching mystery.
    4.  **Observation & Deduction Focus:** Rewards player curiosity, attention to detail, and social intelligence over fast reflexes or combat.

Okay, that feels pretty solid as a starting point. It's definitely outside the typical "Crime & Investigation" box, leans into my intuitive flow, and incorporates several different gameplay styles.

Now, who to consult first? Well, since this is a really free-form idea, I probably want to bounce it off someone else who appreciates creative thinking. Sato and Ito are both General Members like me. Sato's workstyle is Balanced, but Ito is also Intuitive, just like me, and she's a bit older. She might appreciate the "think outside the box" directive and could offer some good initial feedback or help expand on the concept without shutting it down too early. Watanabe is younger and Balanced, he might bring a different perspective, maybe even some technical or gameplay mechanics ideas later, but I think for the initial conceptual phase, Ito might be a better fit to just brainstorm freely with.

Yamamoto, the leader, will eventually summarize, so I'll need to have something more structured for him. But for now, just getting the raw idea out and refined a bit is the goal. I should probably approach Ito first, maybe just casually bring it up, "Hey Ito-san, I've been kicking around some ideas for the new project, thinking way outside the box for 'Crime & Investigation'..." and then see what she thinks. I'd be interested in her immediate reaction and if anything similar has been done, or if she sees any immediate pitfalls or exciting avenues for expansion.
