* **【初期思考】**
> Alright, let's get this brain-dump started. New game idea, huh? And they want us to really think outside the box, even for horror. That's good, I like that. Keeps things fresh. At my age, I've seen enough jump scares and slasher flicks to last a lifetime, so something genuinely *new* would be exciting.

My department is 'Horror Game Planning,' but they said we can mix things up. That's where the real fun begins. Pure horror can be great, but it can also get… predictable. What if we take something totally innocuous and twist it? Like, a life simulation game, but it slowly, *insidiously* turns into horror? Not just like, a demon pops out, but something much more subtle and disturbing.

Hmm, a life simulation… You're just living your everyday life, maybe in a small, cozy town. You manage your relationships, your job, grow a garden, typical stuff. But then, you start noticing things. Small things. Like, people's expressions don't quite match what they're saying. Or objects in your house move when you're not looking, just slightly. A shadow in the corner that isn't supposed to be there. And it slowly escalates. Not with monsters, not at first anyway, but with the *disintegration of reality* around you. You start questioning your own sanity, the sanity of the town. Is it you? Is it them? Is it some unseen force slowly taking hold?

Let's call the concept something like "Suburban Decay" or "The Quiet Corruption."
**Story idea:** You play as a new resident in a seemingly idyllic, isolated town. Your goal is to build a perfect life, make friends, maybe start a family. But the town has a secret, a slow-acting psychological contaminant, maybe even an entity that feeds on complacency and routine. The more comfortable you get, the more susceptible you become. You discover the townspeople are subtly changing, losing parts of themselves, becoming shells, or worse, becoming unwitting agents of something malevolent. The horror comes from losing your own sense of self, your memories, your connections, as the town's true nature slowly consumes everything.

**Core content:**
*   **Life Sim Mechanics:** Day-night cycle, relationship building, job system, home customization, gardening, mini-games. All of this serves to ground the player in the mundane before it's shattered.
*   **Corruption Mechanics:** As the game progresses, visual glitches start appearing – fleeting distortions, incorrect textures, objects morphing. Audio cues become unsettling – whispers, distorted music, familiar sounds played at wrong times. Dialogue from NPCs becomes increasingly unsettling or repetitive. Your character's stats (sanity, memory) would degrade.
*   **Investigation/Mystery:** You'd have to piece together what's happening through subtle clues, hidden journals, strange occurrences, and interactions. The more you investigate, the more danger you're in, but also the more you understand.
*   **Multiple Endings:** Based on how much you resist the corruption, how much you uncover, and who you trust (or don't). Can you escape? Can you save anyone? Or do you become another part of the town's quiet horror?

**Slogan:** "Build your perfect life. Lose your perfect mind." Or maybe, "The perfect life, perfectly corrupted." Something short and punchy that hints at the twist.

**Key Selling Points:**
1.  **Unique Genre Blend:** A genuinely fresh take on horror by integrating deep life simulation, appealing to players who enjoy cozy games but crave a disturbing twist.
2.  **Psychological Horror at its Core:** Focuses on unsettling atmosphere, existential dread, and questioning reality rather than relying solely on jump scares or gore.
3.  **Player Choice and Impact:** Decisions influence the narrative and the mental state of the protagonist, leading to multiple, diverging outcomes.
4.  **Gradual, Insidious Threat:** The horror isn't in a monster you fight, but a pervasive, subtle corruption that slowly eats away at everything you hold dear.

Okay, that's a decent start. It's got the horror, but it's not *just* horror. It plays into the "outside the box" directive pretty well, I think.
