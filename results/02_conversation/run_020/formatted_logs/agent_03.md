But again, that's *after* I've formed some initial thoughts. If it's truly "first," my honest answer is "myself."

Now, **how to approach this task?**

Alright, this is where my reflective style really kicks in.

1.  **Deconstruct the "Historical" aspect:** The department is "Historical Game Planning," but it's explicitly stated as a "broad strategic direction" and I can "incorporate other elements or choose not to do so." This is key. I'm not locked into making a strictly historically accurate simulation. I could go for historical fiction, alternate history, a fantasy world *inspired* by a historical period, or even something completely modern with historical *themes* or *mechanisms*. This is where the "free ideas" really come into play. Maybe I can take a historical event or person and completely twist it, or extract a core societal structure from a historical period and place it in a futuristic setting.

2.  **Brainstorming Core Concepts & Mechanics (No limits!):**
    *   I'll start by just listing keywords, feelings, and interesting gameplay mechanics I've seen or thought of, completely independent of genre first. Things like "player choice matters," "unseen consequences," "emotional storytelling," "asymmetrical multiplayer," "resource management with a twist," "dynamic narrative," "time manipulation," "social deduction," "exploration with discovery," "character development through relationships."
    *   Then, I'll try to connect these abstract ideas with the "historical" potential, however loosely. What if a historical figure had access to modern technology? What if a forgotten historical detail becomes the key to a modern mystery? What if the "history" is actually a future history, or an alien history?
    *   I also need to think about the *experience* I want players to have. Do I want them to feel powerful? Intrigued? Challenged? Nostalgic? Empathetic?

3.  **Developing a Loose Story/Concept:**
    *   Once I have some core ideas for mechanics and an experience, I'll start weaving a very rough narrative thread. It doesn't have to be detailed, just enough to give the game a purpose and a world.
    *   Maybe something like: A puzzle/mystery game set in an alternate Edo period where yokai are real and integrated into society, but a new technology (steam-powered?) is threatening their existence, forcing players to uncover forgotten folklore to save them. Or perhaps a cooperative life-sim where players manage a "lost" historical library that can alter realities through the stories they preserve or rewrite.
    *   The aim isn't perfection, but volume and variety at this stage.

4.  **Contents, Slogan, Key Selling Points:**
    *   Once I have a concept, I'll flesh out what "contents" would entail: specific types of quests, characters, environments, unique systems (e.g., a "reputation" system tied to historical accuracy, or a "truth vs. myth" mechanic).
    *   The slogan should be catchy and encapsulate the game's core hook.
    *   Key selling points will highlight what makes this game unique and appealing. What emotions does it evoke? What problem does it solve for players (e.g., boredom, desire for intellectual challenge, craving for novelty)?

Throughout this process, I'll be jotting down notes, sketching ideas, and probably staring into space a lot, just letting my thoughts connect. It's an iterative process. I'll probably generate a few distinct concepts before I hone in on one or two that I feel are strong enough to discuss with colleagues like Watanabe-san or Ito-san. And eventually, Yamamoto-san, of course, because he's the leader and needs to submit the summary. But the immediate approach is definitely internal, deep thinking.
