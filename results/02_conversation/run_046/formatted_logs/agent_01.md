* **【初期思考】**
> Alright, so here we go again, another game concept. My department's "Post-Apocalyptic Game Planning" is just a suggestion, huh? That's good, because my mind usually jumps all over the place anyway. Intuitive, that's me. Just gotta let the ideas tumble out.

Okay, "who to consult first" and "how to approach this task."

Honestly, for "who to consult first," my gut feeling is usually to just start thinking on my own. I like to get a few raw, unfiltered ideas down before I start bouncing them off people. If I go to someone too early, I might get stuck trying to fit *my* ideas into *their* framework, and that kills the initial spark. So, I guess the very first "consultant" is just... my own brain. I need to spend some time letting the creative juices flow.

But once I've got something, even if it's super rough, who would be good to chat with? I think Sato-san or Takahashi-san would be great. They're both intuitive like me, so they'd probably get where I'm coming from when I'm just throwing out wild, half-baked thoughts. We could just riff off each other, you know? No need for perfectly polished presentations at this stage. Watanabe-san is reflective, so she'd be better for when I have something more concrete and need someone to poke holes in it or help structure it, but for pure idea generation, the intuitive types are where it's at. Yamamoto-san, our leader, I'll definitely loop her in once I have something somewhat coherent, but not for the very first spark. And the other department? Maybe later, if I feel like our concept could benefit from a completely fresh perspective or if it overlaps with what they're doing. But for now, let's keep it in-house, within our post-apocalyptic crew.

Now, "how to approach this task" – the actual design part. Since I'm an intuitive guy, I'm not going to start with a rigid structure. I'll just start daydreaming.

First, I'll think about the core "what if." "Post-Apocalyptic" is too broad. What *kind* of apocalypse? I'm leaning away from typical zombies or nuclear fallout for now. Maybe something more... fantastical? Or deeply psychological? What if the apocalypse wasn't a bang, but a slow fade? Or something that fundamentally changed *humanity* rather than just the environment?

Then, I'll start picturing the *feeling* of the game. Is it desperate and grim? Hopeful? Mysterious? A mix? That emotional core is crucial. From there, I'll let visual and auditory ideas come to mind. What does this world *look* like? What does it *sound* like?

Gameplay style? Completely open, that's exciting. I want something that encourages player agency and discovery. Maybe something that blends genres? Like, a life simulation mixed with mystery, set in a world where everyone's trying to rebuild, but there's a huge secret about *why* the world ended. Or an action-RPG where the "action" isn't just combat, but also complex environmental puzzles and social interactions with strange new factions.

Story-wise, I'm thinking less about a single hero saving the world, and more about emergent narratives. What if the story is about piecing together the past, not just surviving the present? Or about the difficult choices communities have to make when resources are scarce and trust is even scarcer.

Then, I'll think about key selling points. What makes this game different? It needs a hook. It can't just be "another post-apocalyptic game." It has to have a unique twist, a mechanic, or an emotional resonance that stands out. And a slogan... that'll come last, once the concept feels solid. It needs to be catchy and encapsulate the game's essence.

So, yeah, first, I just dive in, let the ideas flow freely without judgment. Then, I'll try to refine those ideas into something coherent, maybe a few bullet points in my head, before I even *think* about talking to Sato-san or Takahashi-san to get their intuitive input and see where their minds go with it. It's all about finding that unique spark.
