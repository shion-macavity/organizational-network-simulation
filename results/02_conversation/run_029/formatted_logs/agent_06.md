> Okay, so I'm Yamamoto, department leader, and my job is to spearhead the concept for this new game project. First things first, I need to get some initial thoughts down, kind of a brain dump, before I even think about talking to anyone. What’s something fresh that still captures the essence of crime and investigation, but isn't just another hard-boiled detective story or a CSI clone?

I’m picturing something that really challenges the player's perception, maybe blurs the line between right and wrong, or even reality and illusion. What if the "crime" isn't a traditional one, or the "investigation" isn't straightforward?

Okay, an idea is bubbling up. How about a game set in a dream world, or a collective subconscious? The "crime" could be a psychic anomaly, a memory wiped, a recurring nightmare taking over, or even a fragmented personality causing chaos. The investigation would involve navigating these dreamscapes, piecing together symbolic clues, confronting dream archetypes, and understanding the emotional core of the "victim" or "perpetrator."

So, the concept could be "Lucid Mind Detective."
**Story:** You play as a "Dream Weaver," someone with the rare ability to enter and manipulate the subconscious minds of others. Your clients come to you with psychological "crimes" – unexplainable anxieties, lost memories that feel stolen, recurring dark visions, or even a mysterious illness stemming from their psyche. You have to journey into their dream worlds, reconstruct their fragmented thoughts, battle their inner demons, and uncover the truth behind their mental struggles. Is it a repressed trauma manifesting? Is someone else psychically interfering? Or is the "crime" an internal conflict the mind is having with itself?

**Contents:**
*   **Gameplay Style:** It could be a blend of a mystery visual novel, a puzzle adventure, and maybe even some light action RPG elements for when you confront manifestations of trauma or external psychic threats.
*   **Exploration:** Each dream world would be unique, surreal, and visually stunning, reflecting the client's personality and their specific "crime." You'd explore these landscapes, interacting with dream fragments, symbolic characters, and environmental puzzles.
*   **Clue Gathering:** Clues wouldn't be physical evidence; they'd be memories, emotions, recurring motifs, and symbolic objects. You'd have a "Dream Journal" to connect these elements.
*   **Decision Making:** Your choices would influence not just the outcome of the investigation, but also the client's mental state, potentially leading to different dream world alterations or even changing *your* perception of reality.
*   **Special Abilities:** As a Dream Weaver, you'd have abilities like "Memory Reconstruction" (piecing together fragmented scenes), "Emotion Resonance" (feeling the echoes of strong emotions from objects or places), or "Dream Manipulation" (altering parts of the dream to access new areas or solve puzzles).

**Slogan:** "Unravel the mind. Solve the unseen." Or maybe, "Where every thought is a clue, and every dream holds a secret." I like that second one more.

**Key Selling Points:**
1.  **Unique Setting:** A truly original "crime" and "investigation" experience set entirely within the psychological landscape of the human mind.
2.  **Deep Narrative & Emotional Impact:** Players don't just solve a case; they delve into profound human experiences, anxieties, and triumphs, offering a rich, emotionally resonant story.
3.  **Creative Puzzle Solving:** Puzzles are integrated into the surreal dream logic, demanding creative thinking beyond typical inventory or logic puzzles.
4.  **Player Agency:** Choices significantly impact the narrative, client outcomes, and even the player's own character development.

Okay, that feels like a solid starting point for "Lucid Mind Detective." It's definitely outside the box and leverages the core departmental genre without being constrained by it.

Now, about who to consult first and how to approach this task... I've got this initial spark, but I need to flesh it out and make sure it has legs. My next step should definitely be to get some other perspectives, especially from the more reflective types in the department. I tend to run with things, so having someone thoughtful to poke holes or suggest practical expansions would be really helpful.

Looking at the list... Sato, Suzuki, Takahashi, and Watanabe are all reflective. Tanaka is also reflective. Ito is intuitive like me.

I think I'll want to bounce this general concept off someone who can think about the structure and potential pitfalls without shutting down the creativity. I don't want to present a fully baked idea yet, just the core "Lucid Mind Detective" concept and see what kind of reactions it gets.

I should probably start with one or two people first, rather than a whole team meeting right away. Maybe Sato-san? She's the most senior general member, and her reflective nature combined with her experience might give me some really insightful feedback on the overall feasibility and narrative depth.
