Sasaki, with her balanced approach, will be invaluable in bridging different viewpoints.

As for 'how to approach this task,' my strategy would be something like this:

First, as I said, a **team brainstorming session.** I'd start by reminding everyone about the complete freedom we have regarding genre and gameplay style. I'd encourage them to think about core experiences they enjoy, unique feelings they want to evoke in players, or perhaps even existing problems they see in current games that we could solve. We need to cast a very wide net initially. I wouldn't put any time limits on this initial phase, just let the ideas flow. I'd make sure to take detailed notes, or have someone else do it, to capture everything.

Once we have a good pool of initial ideas, the next step would be to **identify promising concepts.** This is where our reflective styles will come in handy. We'd look for themes, mechanics, or emotional cores that resonate strongly or seem to have unique potential. We're looking for something that stands out, something that truly embodies "thinking outside the box." We'd probably cluster similar ideas and try to find the strongest thread within each cluster.

From there, we'd start to **flesh out a few top concepts.** For each promising idea, we'd try to develop a basic story premise – a compelling 'what if' scenario. What's the core conflict? Who are the protagonists? What's the overarching goal? Simultaneously, we'd start to imagine the gameplay – what would the player *do*? Is it exploration, problem-solving, combat, social interaction, creation? We'd also consider the 'feeling' of the game – is it exciting, peaceful, mysterious, challenging?

Finally, we'd then focus on **refining one or two core ideas** into a more structured proposal. This would involve crafting a more detailed concept statement, outlining potential contents or features, and then, crucially, coming up with a catchy slogan and those all-important key selling points. What makes this game special? Why would someone want to play it?

I think involving the whole team throughout these stages, with me guiding the discussion and ensuring everyone has a voice, will lead to the strongest possible outcome. We need that collective creativity and diverse thinking to really hit a home run here. I won't hesitate to poke and prod at ideas, not to criticize, but to uncover their fullest potential. And if we hit a wall, I'm open to considering if another department might have a perspective we're missing, though I'd prefer to leverage our internal team's strengths first. For now, it's about getting everyone together and getting those initial sparks flying.
