* **【初期思考】**
> Alright, let's get this thinking process started. The company wants us to come up with something fresh, "outside the box," even if our department is "Military/War Game Planning." That's a good challenge, especially at my age. You see a lot of cycles in this industry, and sometimes the "new" ideas are just old ones with a coat of paint. But this directive feels genuinely open.

My department's genre, Military/War Game, is just a broad strategic direction, they said. That means I don't *have* to make another shoot-em-up or RTS with tanks. I can twist it, explore different angles. The core of "military" or "war" doesn't just mean fighting. It can mean strategy, logistics, the human element, the consequences, the politics, even the *absence* of war as a desired outcome. And with all those gameplay styles available – action, RPG, puzzle, life sim... that's a lot to play with.

I think the best way to start here is to just sit with it myself for a bit. Before I go bouncing ideas off someone, I need to let my own thoughts marinate. I’ve got enough experience to know what works and what usually doesn’t, but also to recognize when something truly different is needed. Just throwing out half-baked concepts to the others isn't my style. I like to have at least a kernel of an idea to discuss. My workstyle is balanced, after all. I value both the creative spark and the grounded reality of execution.

So, how do I approach this? "Military/War Game" but "outside the box." What if we flip the script completely? Instead of focusing on the glory or the action of war, what about the *cost*? Or the *preparation*? Or even the *prevention*?

Let's think. A "Military/War Game" that isn't about direct combat. What about a game focused on intelligence and espionage, but from a deeply personal, human perspective? Like, "The Quiet Front."

The concept could be a narrative-driven mystery/RPG hybrid. You're not a soldier on the front lines, but an intelligence operative, maybe embedded deep within a hostile nation, or even within your *own* nation's bureaucracy, trying to prevent a war. The "military" aspect comes from the high stakes, the national security implications, the tactical gathering of information, the counter-intelligence, but the "war" is fought through whispers, data, and difficult moral choices, rather than bullets.

The story could follow a network of agents, each with their own specializations and vulnerabilities, trying to uncover a conspiracy that's deliberately escalating tensions between two powerful nations. Maybe the conspiracy isn't even external, but internal, driven by profiteers or ideologues within one's own government. The player could take on multiple roles, switching between different operatives, each with their own skill sets for infiltration, decryption, social engineering, or even just observation and pattern recognition.

Content wise, it would involve:
*   **Deep branching narrative:** Player choices have significant, long-term consequences, not just for the mission but for the lives of the agents and the fate of nations.
*   **Puzzle elements:** Decrypting codes, piecing together fragments of information, understanding complex political structures.
*   **Stealth and social simulation:** Infiltrating secure locations, building trust with NPCs, manipulating conversations, understanding cultural nuances. It’s not about fighting guards, but about bypassing them entirely or turning them into unwitting assets.
*   **Resource management (information and trust):** Information is currency. Trust is fragile. Making a wrong move could compromise an asset or expose a network.
*   **Multiple endings:** Based on which clues you uncover, which agents survive, and the ultimate outcome of the conflict – war averted, war started, a new cold war, internal collapse.

The key selling points would be:
1.  **A War Game Without Direct Combat:** Experience the tension and stakes of military conflict through intelligence, diplomacy, and strategy, not violence. It's "chess on a global scale, with human lives as pawns."
2.  **Moral Ambiguity and Player Choice:** Every decision matters. Uncover uncomfortable truths. Whose side are you really on when the lines are blurred?
3.  **Rich, Immersive Narrative:** A spy thriller where you *are* the protagonist(s), weaving through a complex web of lies and allegiances.
4.  **Intellectual Challenge:** Engage your mind with puzzles, deduction, and strategic thinking rather than just reflexes.

The slogan? "The battlefield is in the shadows. The weapon is truth." Or maybe, "Prevent the war. Uncover the truth." I like the second one more, it's more direct about the player's agency. "Prevent the War. Uncover the Truth."

This feels like it takes the "Military/War Game" concept and stretches it in an interesting direction, using elements from mystery, RPG, and even life simulation in terms of relationship building and social interaction. It's definitely "outside the box" from traditional war games.

Now, who would I consult first with this idea? Well, I think Tanaka might appreciate this kind of balanced, strategic approach, given his own workstyle. And Yamamoto, as the leader, would need to hear it eventually. But I also think someone like Takahashi or Suzuki, with their intuitive workstyles, might bring some really wild, creative spins to this core idea, especially on the narrative and character development. Or even Nakamura from Sci-Fi Planning could give it a fresh angle. But I'll refine my own pitch a little more before I actively seek out a conversation. I just wanted to get these initial thoughts down first. This sounds like it could be something
