> Okay, so I need to come up with a game idea – story, concept, contents, slogan, and key selling points. Right, "Superhero Game Planning" is just a broad direction, and I can pull in anything, any genre. That's good, it gives a lot of freedom. My workstyle is 'Reflective', so I should probably take some time to really flesh out an idea before rushing to talk to anyone.

Let's see. Most superhero games are about action, fighting, saving the world in a big, flashy way. What if we explored something different? Something that delves into the *human* side of having powers, the everyday struggles, the internal conflict, rather than just the external battles.

What if the "superhero" element isn't about grand, world-saving events, but about how extraordinary abilities impact an *ordinary* life? That feels like it could be fresh. A 'life simulation' angle with a superhero twist. Yeah, I like that. It plays into the genre but subverts expectations.

So, the core **concept** could be: "Beyond the Cape: A slice-of-life simulation where you navigate the mundane world with extraordinary secret powers." It's not about being 'the hero' the world expects, but about being *you* with a secret that changes everything.

For the **story**, let's say the player character is someone completely unremarkable – maybe a college student struggling with grades, or someone stuck in a dead-end job, living in a quiet, normal city. One day, something happens – a strange accident, a dormant gene activates – and they develop a superpower. But it's not necessarily a flashy, destructive one. Maybe it's something subtle, even inconvenient, at first. The story would revolve around their struggle to keep this power a secret from friends, family, and employers, while also learning to control it and deciding what to do with it. Do they use it for personal gain? Do they secretly help people in small ways? Do they try to suppress it and just live a normal life? Choices would branch, leading to different outcomes for their personal life and the quiet community around them. It could be about finding your own definition of 'heroism'.

**Contents (Gameplay Mechanics):**
*   **Life Simulation Core:** We'd have typical life sim elements like managing daily schedules (work, studies, hobbies), building and maintaining relationships (friendships, romance, family dynamics) through dialogue choices and shared activities, and managing resources like money, energy, and mental stress.
*   **Secret Power System:** The player would have a specific, perhaps initially uncontrollable, superpower. It wouldn't be about endless combat, but about how and when to subtly use the power. Using it could have consequences:
    *   **"Attention" Meter:** Using powers publicly or recklessly would increase an "Attention" meter. Too high, and people start noticing, asking questions, maybe even a local blogger or conspiracy theorist gets on your trail.
    *   **"Power Drain/Stress" Meter:** Using powers could consume energy or cause mental strain. Overusing them might lead to negative mood debuffs, exhaustion, or even temporary loss of control.
    *   **Skill Tree for Powers:** Players could "train" their power, unlocking new ways to use it more subtly, efficiently, or effectively in non-combat scenarios (e.g., using telekinesis to silently fix something, super-speed to arrive on time for work, minor mind-control to ace an interview).
*   **Moral Dilemmas & Choices:** Players would face decisions that challenge their morality – using powers for personal gain vs. helping others discreetly, protecting their secret vs. doing the right thing. These choices would impact their relationships, reputation, and the overall narrative.
*   **Mini-Games:** Small, contextual mini-games could represent using powers in daily life – precision puzzles for telekinesis, quick-time events for super-speed tasks, memory games for empathy/mind-reading.
*   **Relationship System:** Relationships with NPCs would evolve based on choices, revealing secrets, and helping them (or not) with powers. Different relationship paths could lead to different endings.

The **slogan** could be something like: "What if your greatest power was keeping a secret?" or "Your secret identity isn't a mask, it's your life." Maybe, "Superpowers: Great for saving the world, terrible for a nine-to-five." I think the second one is a bit more evocative and speaks to the core idea. "Your secret identity isn't a mask, it's your life." Or even simpler, "Live a normal life. With a secret." Yeah, that's punchy. "Live a normal life. With a secret."

And for the **key selling points**:
1.  **Unique "Superhero" Experience:** Not about fighting villains, but about the deeply personal and relatable challenges of having secret powers in an ordinary life.
2.  **Rich Narrative Choices:** A branching story driven by player decisions, impacting relationships, career, and the fate of their local community.
3.  **Engaging Life Simulation:** Balance daily routines, cultivate relationships, and manage personal well-being alongside the demands of a secret identity.
4.  **Subtle Power Mechanics:** Explore a fresh take on superpowers, focusing on their practical, often inconvenient, applications and the struggle to keep them hidden.

Okay, that feels pretty solid for a first pass. It definitely goes beyond the typical superhero game, and it leans into the idea of "outside the box." My next step would be to figure out who might be good to discuss this with. Given that I'm reflective, I like to have my thoughts pretty organized before I engage. Maybe Suzuki or Takahashi for an intuitive perspective on the concept, or Tanaka for a balanced viewpoint to see if it resonates. Yamamoto, as the leader, would be for a later stage, once the idea is a bit more refined.

But for now, this internal thought process is complete for the initial brainstorming. I've got my game idea.
