## ■ ターン 0: 初期状態
* **【初期思考】**
> Alright, so here we are, another new project, huh? "Free ideas," they said. "Think outside the box." Well, that's right up my alley. Being in "Historical Game Planning" usually means a lot of research and sticking to facts, but this time, it seems like they're really giving us room to play. My brain's already buzzing with a few things, trying to find a hook.

I mean, "historical" is broad, right? We don't have to do another samurai game or Roman empire epic. What if we took the *essence* of history – the stories, the people, the forgotten places – and just twisted it into something new? My intuitive side is really pushing for something unexpected.

Okay, so picture this: Instead of fighting *in* history, what if we fought *for* history, but in a really abstract, almost mystical way? What if the core concept revolves around the idea that history isn't just about dates and events, but it's a living, breathing tapestry of stories, emotions, and forgotten memories?

My idea is a game called something like "Echoes of Aethelgard." It's an RPG, but with heavy puzzle and life-simulation elements woven in. The story would be about a world where 'memories' of the past, actual historical moments, are literally fading and breaking apart due to some cosmic phenomenon or a subtle, insidious force. Not evil, per se, but like a slow decay.

The player character isn't a warrior or a general; they're a "Memory Weaver." They have this unique ability to enter fragmented historical 'echoes' – not time travel, but almost like dreamscapes or spirit realms of past eras. Each echo is a miniature, self-contained world based on a real or imagined historical period. Think a bustling marketplace in ancient Persia, a quiet monastic scriptorium in medieval Europe, or a serene tea house in Edo-period Japan.

The goal? To find the core 'memory shards' within these echoes. These aren't just objects; they're emotional anchors, significant events, or deeply personal stories of people from that time. You interact with NPCs who are essentially spectral remnants of people from that era. You don't fight them in traditional combat. Instead, you solve environmental puzzles, piece together narratives from clues, or even perform small, meaningful tasks for these 'echoes' – like helping a baker recreate a lost recipe, or reuniting two lost lovers through their letters. It's about empathy and understanding.

The gameplay loop would involve:
1.  **Exploring Echoes:** Each echo is a beautifully rendered, semi-open puzzle box. You'd need to observe, listen, and interact.
2.  **Memory Weaving:** This is where the life-sim part comes in. You collect 'threads' – snippets of stories, cultural details, even sensory data like tastes or sounds – and then use a crafting-like system back in your 'Sanctuary' (your hub, a personal space that grows with your understanding) to literally *re-weave* the fragmented memory shards. This might involve matching historical details, understanding emotional connections, or solving logic puzzles.
3.  **Restoring the Tapestry:** Successfully re-weaving a memory shard strengthens the overall 'Tapestry of Time,' visually represented by beautiful, growing artwork in your Sanctuary.

The "contents" would be diverse historical settings, each with its own unique visual style, musical score, and puzzle mechanics. We could have dozens of these echoes. Think "Journey" meets "Stardew Valley" but with historical context and a deep, emotional narrative.

My slogan for this? "Echoes of Aethelgard: Reclaim the Past, One Story at a Time."
And the key selling points?
*   **Emotionally Resonant Storytelling:** A focus on empathy, discovery, and the human condition across ages.
*   **Unique Puzzle-RPG Hybrid:** Combines engaging environmental puzzles with a satisfying 'memory weaving' mechanic and character progression focused on understanding.
*   **Visually Stunning Historical Immersion:** Explore diverse, richly detailed historical settings that come alive through art and sound.
*   **Relaxing, Thought-Provoking Gameplay:** A slower, more introspective experience compared to action-heavy titles, appealing to players who love narrative and exploration.

Now, who to consult first? Given my intuitive style, I've just blasted out a bunch of ideas, probably without much regard for practicality yet. I definitely need someone to bounce this off of and help me structure it a bit.

Yamaguchi-san, as the leader, I'd probably need to brief him eventually, but he might want something more refined. And Nakamura-san is reflective, so she'd be good for detailed feedback once I have a clearer picture, but she might pick apart the initial wildness too much. Sasaki-san has a balanced style, so he might be good, but...

You know, I think Kobayashi-kun would be a good first stop. He's also intuitive like me, so he'd probably get the bigger picture and the feeling I'm going for without getting bogged down immediately. We could just brainstorm wildly together, maybe even build on each other's crazy ideas, before trying to rein it in. He'd probably be excited by the genre-bending aspect. Yeah, I think I'll try catching Kobayashi-kun first. Just to see if he thinks it's as cool as I do, and maybe we can push the concept even further, or find the biggest holes in it together before bringing it to others. We can refine it a bit, then present a more solid-ish crazy idea to the others.
