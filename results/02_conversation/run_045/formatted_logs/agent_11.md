My department is 'Horror Game Planning,' but the prompt says we can really stretch that definition, even incorporate other elements or disregard it entirely. That's liberating. "Horror" for me doesn't just mean jump scares or gore; it can be psychological, existential, the horror of the unknown, or even the subtle dread of societal pressures. And with my age, I've seen so many trends come and go, so I'm not tied to any single style.

Okay, so "who to consult first" and "how to approach this task."

Honestly, my first consultation is always with myself. I need to get some initial thoughts down, some raw concepts, before I even think about bouncing ideas off someone else. If I go in too early, my ideas will be half-baked, and it won't be productive. I need to develop at least one or two distinct directions first.

My approach will be to start broadly. What kind of feelings do I want to evoke? What kind of narrative experience do I find compelling? Then, how can that be translated into gameplay?

Given the open nature, I'm thinking... what if we leaned into a different kind of "horror"? Not necessarily monsters, but something more... unsettling. The horror of choice, perhaps, or the horror of a mundane reality twisted just enough to be terrifying.

Let's try a blend. Maybe something like a life simulation or a puzzle game, but with a growing sense of dread.

Here's an initial thought brewing in my head:

**Concept:** A seemingly idyllic, cozy life simulation game where players manage a small, charming business – let's say a quaint antique shop or a bespoke clock repair shop – in a picturesque, isolated town. The "horror" isn't external, but internal and environmental. As the game progresses, subtle anomalies start appearing. Items in the shop move on their own, customers give increasingly bizarre requests or share unsettling stories, the town itself starts to feel...off. The player's sanity or perception slowly degrades, making them question what's real and what's not. The goal is to either maintain a semblance of normalcy and sanity, or to uncover the dark secret of the town, at great personal cost.

**Story:** The protagonist inherits a family business in a town called "Oakhaven," known for its timeless charm and quirky residents. Initially, it's a peaceful experience: fixing old watches, polishing antique trinkets, chatting with friendly customers. But soon, the protagonist finds cryptic messages carved into furniture, hears whispers from empty rooms, or notices faces in the reflection of polished surfaces that aren't there. The "friendly" customers might start asking for items that shouldn't exist, or items that seem to hold a piece of the town's unsettling history. Dreams become vivid and disturbing, bleeding into waking life. The overarching mystery is tied to Oakhaven itself, perhaps a forgotten pact, a curse, or a sentient entity residing within the town's very fabric, slowly consuming its inhabitants' minds and memories.

**Contents (Gameplay Elements):**
*   **Business Management:** Repairing items with mini-games, managing inventory, pricing, customer interactions.
*   **Exploration:** Exploring the shop and eventually parts of Oakhaven.
*   **Puzzle Solving:** Deciphering clues, solving environmental puzzles tied to the town's history or the anomalies.
*   **"Sanity" System:** A subtle meter that affects perception, visual filters, sound design, and the reliability of information. Lower sanity might make tasks harder, or reveal hidden truths.
*   **Dynamic Events:** Random events like items appearing/disappearing, strange noises, customer oddities.
*   **Multiple Endings:** Depending on whether the player succumbs to the madness, escapes the town, or uncovers and confronts the source of the horror.

**Slogan:** "Welcome to Oakhaven. Some things are better left undisturbed." or "The quaint shop in the quiet town. Some secrets refuse to stay buried."

**Key Selling Points:**
1.  **Unique Blend of Genres:** A cozy life sim experience slowly descends into psychological horror, offering something fresh.
2.  **Subtle, Atmospheric Terror:** Focus on dread, paranoia, and existential unease rather than cheap jump scares.
3.  **Engaging Mystery:** A deep, unfolding narrative mystery tied to a charming yet sinister town.
4.  **Player Choice and Consequence:** Decisions impact the protagonist's sanity and the game's outcome.
5.  **High Replayability:** Multiple paths and endings encourage players to experience the town's secrets differently.

Now, this is just a starting point. It feels like it fits my "reflective" nature, allowing for deep narrative and atmospheric building. It also pushes the definition of "horror" in an interesting way.

Once I've refined this a bit more in my head, thinking about specific mechanics and how the horror escalates, then I might consider who to talk to. Yamaguchi, our leader, is intuitive, so a strong, evocative concept like this might appeal to her directly. Kobayashi, also reflective, might appreciate the nuanced horror and the deeper psychological elements, perhaps we could brainstorm how to implement the sanity system or the narrative threads. Yoshida and Sasaki, being balanced, would likely want to hear about the core loop and potential market appeal, which I'll need to think about more too.

But for now, I'll keep fleshing out Oakhaven in my mind. What kind of antique items could hold the most disturbing stories? What kind of bizarre requests would customers make? Yes, this is a good start.
