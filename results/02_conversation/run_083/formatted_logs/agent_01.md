I think she'd appreciate a well-thought-out idea and could help me refine it with her own insights. We're both a bit older too, so we might have a common understanding of certain life experiences that could inform a game.

Now, as for "how to approach this task" itself, this is where my reflective nature really comes into play.

My main approach will be to start broad, then narrow it down.
1.  **Deconstruct "Slice of Life" and "Think Outside the Box":** I'm going to start by thinking about the essence of "slice of life" – the quiet moments, the relationships, the everyday joys and challenges, personal growth, community. But then I'll immediately challenge myself: what if those "slices" are set in a completely unexpected place? What if the everyday life is happening in a high-fantasy world, or a cyberpunk future, or even on a space station? What if the core "slice" isn't human? This freedom is a golden opportunity to reimagine the genre.
2.  **Brainstorm Core Concepts & Emotions:** Instead of immediately thinking "game mechanics," I want to focus on the feeling I want to evoke. Is it comfort? Nostalgia? A sense of discovery? Empathy? I'll think about a central theme or a unique twist that could make our "slice of life" game stand out. It could be about finding beauty in mundane tasks, or about the quiet heroism of everyday people.
3.  **Develop a Seed of a Story/World:** Once I have a core concept or emotion, I'll start building a very basic story premise or world. Who are the characters? What's their daily rhythm? What's the central conflict or journey, however small it might be? It doesn't need to be an epic quest; it could be something like a character trying to perfect a certain dish, or nurture a difficult garden, or connect with a reclusive neighbor.
4.  **Connect to Gameplay Elements:** Only then will I start considering how this story or concept translates into gameplay. If it's about connecting with people, perhaps it's a cooperative puzzle game where you solve social dilemmas. If it's about nurturing, maybe it's a life simulation game with complex emotional depth. The genre isn't fixed, so I'm open to anything – even something that seems completely opposite to traditional "slice of life" on the surface, but maintains that core emotional resonance.
5.  **Refine Slogan and Selling Points:** After I have a more concrete concept, story, and some initial ideas for content, I'll work on a slogan that encapsulates the unique selling proposition. The key selling points will naturally flow from the core concept and what makes it special. I'll try to focus on the emotional impact and the unique experience, rather than just technical features.

I really need that initial quiet time to allow creative ideas to surface without immediate judgment. I'll make sure my ideas are detailed enough in terms of story, concept, and content before I even think about bringing them to Yamamoto-san, or even to Sato-san, for that matter. I want to present something coherent and well-considered.
