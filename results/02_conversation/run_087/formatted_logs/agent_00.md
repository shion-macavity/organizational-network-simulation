> Alright, let's get my thoughts in order for this project. It's a fresh start, which is always exciting. Designing a new game from scratch, with no real limits on genre or concept, that's the kind of challenge I really enjoy at my age. "Modern Setting" is just a suggestion, they said, and we can really go wild. I like that flexibility.

First things first, I need to get some initial ideas brewing in my own head. Just sit down with a cup of tea and let my mind wander. What kind of stories haven't been told lately? What kind of experiences are people looking for beyond the usual shoot-em-ups or fantasy epics? Given my years, I've seen so many trends come and go, but some core human desires in games always remain: connection, discovery, mastery, escapism.

I'm thinking maybe something that leverages a modern setting not for action, but for connection or problem-solving. A cooperative puzzle game, perhaps, set in a bustling city or even a quiet suburban neighborhood, where players have to work together using modern tools—like social media, smart devices, or even public transport networks—to solve a larger mystery or achieve a community goal. The "mystery" part could be uncovering a local legend, or finding a lost pet that's become a town mascot, or even solving a series of whimsical environmental puzzles.

For the story, it could be about a group of diverse neighbors who initially don't know each other well, but are brought together by a shared, unusual event. Maybe a series of strange, small "glitches" in their everyday modern lives – like streetlights turning off at odd times, or strange coded messages appearing on public screens, or even plants growing unusually fast in public parks. They realize these aren't random, but part of a larger, gentle puzzle orchestrated by something or someone benevolent, maybe an eccentric inventor or an AI trying to communicate.

The concept would be "Community Connection & Everyday Enchantment." It's about finding magic and meaning in the mundane, and the power of human connection in an increasingly digital world. The contents would include a series of interconnected mini-games or challenges that require communication and teamwork. For example, one player might need to "scan" a QR code in the game world to get a clue, while another has to interpret a riddle from a park bench, and a third needs to coordinate their in-game avatar to be at a specific location at a specific time, all contributing to a larger objective.

A slogan... hmm, something like: "Discover the Magic Next Door." Or "Unite Your Neighborhood, Unravel the Ordinary."

Key selling points would be: unique cooperative gameplay fostering real-world communication skills (among players), a heartwarming story about community, charming art style, and a refreshingly gentle pace compared to most modern games. It's less about high stakes and more about warm, fuzzy feelings and clever problem-solving.

Now, about who to talk to first. Once I have this rough outline, I think I'll want to get some initial reactions.
Young Tanaka, with his reflective workstyle, might offer some really insightful critiques or build on the logical aspects of the puzzles. He's young, so he'd know what resonates with a newer generation in terms of modern setting elements. I value a thoughtful perspective like his.

Then there's Takahashi. He's got that intuitive workstyle, similar to my own in some ways, but often comes up with completely out-of-the-box ideas. He might take my "everyday enchantment" and really push the boundaries into something truly unique, maybe suggesting a mystical element I hadn't considered. His experience and age are also close to mine, so we might have a similar wavelength.

I'll definitely need to eventually loop in Yamamoto, our leader, but I'd like to refine the idea a bit more with my colleagues first. Get some positive momentum and constructive feedback before presenting it to him. He's younger, but balanced, so he'll appreciate a well-thought-out, yet creative, proposal.

And Nakamura from the Superhero department... That's an interesting thought. She's also 62 and balanced. It might be good to get an outside perspective, especially since her department focuses on something so different. Maybe she'll have an idea of how to subtly inject a "superhero-like" communal effort into my "everyday enchantment" concept, or just provide a fresh take on audience appeal. But I think I'll start within my own department first, get the core idea solid, and then maybe reach out to Nakamura if I feel like I need a different kind of spark.

So, the approach is: brainstorm solo first, then discuss with Tanaka and Takahashi to expand and refine, and finally, present a more polished concept to Yamamoto.
